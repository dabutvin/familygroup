<h2 id="old-country">From the old country to Bayonne</h2>

Grandpa Isaac Aaron Sklar was on his way to Argentina to join his wife’s brothers who had emigrated there. His own brothers, Jacob (Yankiel) and Louis (Leybish), had recently immigrated to Bayonne, New Jersey. They wrote him letters and persuaded him to come join them in Bayonne. He arrived there in 1906 and moved in with his brother Yankiel on Avenue C near 24th St. Grandma Celia and all the children joined him in 1910.

When the Zeyta left for America, Bubbe Celia moved in with her sister-in-law, Liba, and her family across the river in Damur. The Bubbe had a fur-lined cape which she gave up for money to live on. Isaac Sklar arrived in America and moved in with his brother Jacob and his family at 540 Avenue C. He is listed with them in the 1910 Federal census as “Iky Skla.”

The family left by train for Lebau in Latvia, a long trip at that time. Each of the older children was responsible for a younger one. Uncle Max was Sophie’s responsibility. Sophie faked her age and also hid in the luggage racks and berths to save the cost of the ticket. They left Lebau, Latvia on the SS *Russia* on August 30, 1910 and arrived in New York City on September 11, 1910 and moved in with Louis and Dora Sklar. The ship manifest lists Zippa Schkliar, 40; Wulf, 16; Nechema, 14; Yael, 4; Mendel, 3; Maria, 11; Schifra, 10; Liba, 7.

<h2 id="russia">The Sklars in Russia</h2>

The Sklars came from Dnepropetrovsk in the Ukraine, Russia. It lies on the Dnieper River, the third longest river in Europe. At the time they left it was still called Ekaterinoslav. Liba Sklar, the Zeyta’s sister, and his parents Benzion and Mariasha Sklar lived across the Dnieper River in Danmur or Aframur. Benzion Sklar ran a lumber mill and had many men in his employ. Liba and her husband ran a grocery store or a flour mill. It was suburban. Liba lived through World War I.

Benzion Sklar would hire young men to work his lumber mill by first inviting them to lunch. The young men who finished lunch quickest would be the first hired, since he figured less time would be taken from the workday by those men.

Louis—Meyer Leibish Sklar, the youngest of Benzion’s children—was the first to immigrate to the United States. He was followed shortly thereafter by Yankiel—Jacob—and finally by Isaac. His other children lived out their lives in Russia.

Lipa—Lyov, an older son—died in his late thirties of brain fever, probably before 1898. Uncle Leon Sklar, born in 1898, was likely named for this uncle, who in all probability died around that time—his birth date being in the 1860s. Lyov was a highly trained man who had gone through the gymnasium. He was a renowned scientist and inventor in Russia and had an active correspondence with Thomas A. Edison in West Orange and Menlo Park, New Jersey in the latter part of the 19th century. He invented something to do with musical sounds. He was married to a general’s daughter and a representative of the Czar attended their wedding. They had four sons.

Moses and Noah were the two other children of Benzion and Mariasha Sklar. Noah Sklar passed away before Sophie left for America in 1910. Shifra Sklar, for whom Sophie was named, was Benzion’s mother.

Zeyta Isaac Sklar had in his youth met and fallen in love with a girl. She was beautiful, with long hair, a gorgeous voice, and had been a talented guitar player. She had also been in love with someone else before the Zeyta married her. She married the Zeyta and they lived together for about a month when her lover returned, approached the Zeyta, and threatened his life. The marriage was ended.

Isaac and Celia lived in the city of Dnepropetrovsk in an apartment. Grandpa Isaac was an excellent mechanic and worked repairing parts on the railroad. There were several pogroms and Grandpa Isaac was forced to do guard duty. His brother Lipa’s wife fled to the Zeyta’s house with her four sons. They remained in Russia. One of the sons became an officer in the Russian army and in later years made contact with the American relatives. The mother was a very slim and attractive woman, well dressed.

<h2 id="celia-family">Celia’s Dunn family</h2>

Grandma Celia (Tsippa) Dunn Sklar had two older brothers, William and Zissel Dunn, and one younger brother, Samuel Dunn. They lived in the same area. One Pesach night, after the second Seder, William Sklar and his family were already in bed when a noise was heard. A man had broken in and was stealing a silver sugar bowl and creamer. His wife took the older daughter, Sonia, and escaped; William, trying to save Rachel, the younger daughter, was killed on the spot, leaving the widow and the two small daughters. Later, his brother Zissel adopted the oldest daughter and immigrated with his wife to Buenos Aires, Argentina. Zissel and his wife had no children of their own. This incident started the *bubbe-meise* in the family that no matter how tired one is, every knife will be put in a drawer after each meal—never leave a sharp knife loose on a table—the intruder had stabbed William to death with a knife that had been left out on the table.

The younger brother, Samuel, was drafted into military service and was very unhappy. Ultimately he was able to position himself where he could sneak across a bridge into another country (probably Austria, Hungary, or Romania). From there he got to France, where he worked for Dreyfuss Co., the famous brokerage house. He also met and married a French Jewess and immigrated to Buenos Aires, Argentina. In the late 1940s he and his family visited in Bayonne with the Sklar family group.

<h2 id="brothers-america">Louis, Jacob, and the brothers in America</h2>

Louis Sklar, the youngest brother, was educated in the Gymnasium, spoke Hebrew as well as Yiddish fluently, and was a versatile musician—said to have perfect pitch. He was drafted at 18 into the Czar’s Army, where he played first violin in the orchestra. He also gave music lessons on the side to children of local residents. One day a student’s father warned him of trouble coming for the Jews and offered to help. This man transported goods for the Russian Army during the Russo-Japanese War. At great personal risk, Uncle Louis was transported in one of the trucks to the border and escaped from Russia. He had enough money to reach Frankfurt am Main in Germany, where he stayed four months, then England and Petticoat Lane, the Jewish section of London. He stayed there six months as a fish wrapper—wrapping fried fish in newspaper for transport—then left for the United States.

Louis Sklar’s first jobs in America were sewing men’s trousers in a factory sweatshop and selling newspapers under the El. He was fired from the first—it was a poor job. Uncle Jacob Sklar worked with a man in the bedspring factory named Mendel Miller. Mendel went to the docks to welcome his wife, five or six children, sister and brother, and asked Jacob to come along with his brother Louis, who was 20. The sister was a girl of 18 named Dora. Uncle Louis thought she was the most beautiful girl he had ever seen. They met often and were married five years later. The brother, unfortunately, had a rash and was sent back to Europe.

Uncle Jacob Sklar was the second son to arrive in America. He was married in Russia to Aunt Gitla, who died after giving birth to the twins Leon and Robert Sklar. He married shortly thereafter to Aunt Bluma, with whom he had five children: Nathan, Lena/Leona, Eleanor, Oskar/Harry, and Benjamin. Jacob was a dapper man whose first job in America was in a bedspring factory. Later he went into construction. In the 1920s he owned the Princess Apartments in Jersey City and 84 West 25th Street in Bayonne, where many of the Sklar family lived at one time or another.

<h3 id="metlife-pouch">Metropolitan Life pouch</h3>

A **Metropolitan Life** envelope pouch survives from the era—presented **To Isaac Sklar** with the compliments of **William Sklar**, a small artifact of father, son, and the insurance economy on the block.

![Met Life policy pouch — To Isaac Sklar, from William Sklar](/family-stories/metlife-pouch-isaac-william-sklar.jpg)

Louis Siegal and his wife Elizabeth were friends of the family who lived in Newark. They would come to Bayonne to visit, and one time brought their friend Aunt Dorothy (then Botwinick) and introduced her to Uncle Nathan Sklar. Aunt Dorothy was 21 at the time of their marriage. Uncle Nathan and Aunt Dorothy were married at the Clinton Manor in Newark, NJ on May 25, 1918. In 1919 they moved to 46th Street and Avenue C, Bayonne—the house where Rita and Mickey Slootsky lived years later.

Aunt Mary met Uncle Sam Berlin through a *landsman*. The *landsman* had a tailor shop in Bayonne and took in Uncle Sam as a tailor. Aunt Mary went there with the Bubbe one day and met him. He asked her out; after two dates he proposed; she said yes—they were married immediately. Aunt Mary and Uncle Sam Berlin were married in April 1918; she was then living on West 24th St, Bayonne.

Gussie Bernstein and Willie Sklar met through cousins.

Louis Polevoy was a friend of Willie Sklar. Sophie met him when she was 10; at 16 she had her first date with him and they married about two years later. Louis had to ask the Bubbe’s permission before he could ask Sophie for a date. They had to be home early. They went to the Lyric Theater—later the Opera House on 26th St. and Ave C, Bayonne—where for ten cents you could see a movie and five acts of vaudeville. Sophie was married in a rented gown—$3 for the rental and you could keep the veil. She was married from her home and the Bubbe made all the food. There were four weddings in one year: Willie and Gussie (Bernstein) in November 1917; Nathan and Dorothy on May 25, 1918; Mary and Sam Berlin on November 29, 1918; and Sophie and Louis Polevoy on December 25, 1918.

Becky Okun Rabinowitz was a cousin on Zeyta Isaac’s side. They had a jewelry store on Canal Street in New York City. Itchik Chiam Okun was possibly Becky’s father. Becky was a blood relative.

David Barkoff and his son Sidney Barkoff lived in Boro Park, Brooklyn—cousins.

Emmanuel Sklar, the second child of Willie and Gussie Sklar, died at age five. He had not been normal from birth and had never spoken.

<h2 id="children-marry">Work, heartache, and the next generation</h2>

The Zeyta Isaac Sklar had a heart attack and moved in with Aunt Mary and Uncle Sam Berlin at 729 Avenue C, Bayonne, in 1932, about the time Arnold Berlin was born. It was an ideal arrangement: Aunt Mary went to work and there was a built-in babysitter.

Nathan, at 14, came down with a fever. They lived close to Isaac and Celia Sklar at the time. A doctor was called but too late—he died of appendicitis. Harry Sklar became a dermatologist in Huntington, Long Island; he died in early middle age of heart disease. Ben Sklar, a severe diabetic, earned a doctorate in health care from the University of Michigan, stayed in Michigan, married Hilda, raised two daughters, and served the State of Michigan Health Department. Leona married Charles Rosenkrantz, who owned a “candy store” on Avenue C between 25th and 26th St. on the east side in Bayonne. Charles collapsed and died of a heart attack on the night of Kay Sklar Furst’s wedding. Their son Herbert owned an advertising agency in Jersey City for many years before opening two mortuaries—Menorah Chapels—in Millburn and Teaneck. Herb helped bring down the cost of the “Jewish funeral” in metropolitan New Jersey. Herb’s son Mark Ross has a monument business; his daughter Karen is a mortician in the business with Herb. Diabetes was most prevalent in this branch of the family. Eleanor and her husband Robert Freedman were childless and lived for many years next door to her sister Leona Rosenkrantz at 84 West 25th St. in Bayonne. Gussie Bernstein Sklar’s widow years were spent in an apartment down the hall from Eleanor and Leona—the same house where the Sklar families all lived in the 1920s.

Jacob Sklar died of a heart attack in 1935. Bluma died in 1940. The diabetic strain is from Bluma’s family.

Both Sophie and Aunt Mary had great respect and fond memories of Miss Hayes, an early teacher who was very kind to these two pre-teens placed in a primary school class because they knew no English. One of her first reading lessons in English was “Fanny has a fan, has Fanny a fan.”

Grandpa Willie Sklar—once, looking for a job, he met a man who said he needed overalls to get hired. The man took money to buy them. Grandpa Willie waited. No overalls, no money, no job. Later he met the man again, demanded his money back—and got it.

Sophie and Mary went to work; Sophie at 14 (1914). Uncle Nathan found them jobs in a bookbinding factory in New York City. They earned $5 a week; $1 for car fare and $4 went home to the Bubbe—Grandma Celia Sklar. At that time shirts cost $2 apiece. Aunt Mary stayed at that job until she married. Sophie wanted more money and found other jobs—up to $7 a week—then was laid off. She landed a job in Jersey City, six days a week.

<h2 id="marilyn-tree">Beatrice and Irving</h2>

**Beatrice Sklar**—one of Isaac and Celia’s seven children—married **Irving Pinchuk** on June 7, 1925 in Bayonne (she was born 1902 in Russia; he was born 1897 in Russia/Poland). Their children were **Charlotte** (August 3, 1926), **Marilyn** (February 14, 1931), and **Marty** (January 1, 1940). **Irving’s parents** were Mordechai Pinchuk and Chayasara (Russia); Mordechai had a large family from two marriages, and Irving was one of many siblings. **Beatrice** and **Irving** were **MaBeatty** and **Pop** to the family.

### “Are you a bird or a flower?”

It was something the family said to one another—a playful question.

MaBeatty was a bird—she could sing beautifully. Pop was a flower. Charlotte, Marty, and Marily were flowers.

<h3 id="ace-thread">Ace Thread</h3>

**Irving Pinchuk** had a thread-and-notions store in New York called **Ace Thread**. He carried a thread named for his wife and daughters—**BMC**: **B**eatrice, **M**arilyn, **C**harlotte. Irving lived in **Brownsville**, Brooklyn, before he met **Beatrice Sklar**; then he moved to **Bayonne**. The Manhattan shop was at **72 Madison Avenue**.

<h3 id="bayonne-1940"><em>Bayonne Times</em> — “1940 Baby Derby”</h3>

On **Saturday, January 6, 1940**, the ***Bayonne Times*** ran a full-page **“1940 Baby Derby”**: merchants offered gifts to the first baby of the year born to Bayonne parents. **Martin Pinchuk**—born **January 1, 1940** at 10:45 p.m. at City Hospital to **Mr. & Mrs. Irving Pinchuk**, **106 West 48th Street**—was celebrated as that baby. The page is a snapshot of Depression-era Bayonne retail: **Sklar’s Kiddy Shop** (519 Broadway at 24th), **Sam Henne** wholesale grocer at **741 Avenue C**, coal yards, furniture houses, and more.

They vacationed in Miami Beach; they walked the boardwalk and she held his arm. He would not look at another woman. One woman asked if they were together; MaBeatty pulled him close.

In Boca, MaBeatty loved to shop alone. They flew down and took the bus. She would get on the bus to the shops; Pop worried she’d get lost, so he caught the next bus and followed her.

Artie and MaBeatty played gin—the loser took the trash out. Artie would lose on purpose. They had such a good time.

<h2 id="snapshots">Marilyn and Fred’s years</h2>

**Fred Butvinik** and **Marilyn Pinchuk** had three children in New Jersey:

- **Cheryl** — 1954 (Bayonne)  
- **Bonnie** — 1958 (Paramus)  
- **Warren** — 1960 (Paramus)  

**Warren Butvinik**’s Hebrew name honors **William Sklar**. He and **Kathy Dominick** had **Karen** (1986), **Jen** (1988), and **Dan** (1989).

They bought a house in Paramus, kept going back to the Shore, and stayed tied to Bayonne—where Marilyn had grown up and where the uptown shul and Avenue C still framed the family’s streets.

**787 Pascack Rd, Paramus, NJ 07652** — the house Marilyn and Fred bought in **1956 for $21,000**.

Belmar beach—a lot of people from Bayonne went to Bradley and Belmar. *Evelyn Cohn (Butvinik), Joe Kohn, and Fred Butvinik.*

![Belmar beach — Evelyn Cohn (Butvinik), Joe Kohn, and Fred Butvinik](/family-stories/notes-belmar-beach.jpeg)

Ocean Grove once posted “No Jews” and chained the street Saturday nights so no one would drive through on Sundays—a remembered detail of the Shore in another era.

**124 W. 49th Street** is where Marilyn grew up. She walked to Washington Elementary at 191 Avenue B, Bayonne. In grammar school she had to read from the New Testament and say the Lord’s Prayer every day.

**Temple Ohab Sholom** was Bayonne’s **“Uptown Synagogue.”** It was founded in **1915** as a religious society and became a full-fledged congregation in **1919**, on **Avenue C near 48th Street**, Bayonne, NJ. Marilyn remembered it as **Ohab Sholem** at 1020 Avenue C between 48th and 49th, with a hall—same uptown congregation, the shul her family knew.

<h2 id="knickerbocker">Knickerbocker &amp; the Polevoys</h2>

*Sophie Sklar married Louis Polevoy. Their sons and grandchildren built a bedding business that outlasted the horse-drawn era.*

Founded in 1919, Knickerbocker began with **Richard Polevoy’s** grandparents, **Sophie and Louis Polevoy**, selling bed springs from a horse-drawn carriage in the New York metro area. In the 1950s, the couple’s children expanded the line to folding beds, headboards, and metal bed frames—and helped pioneer bed frames and support systems as a category.

In the 1960s Knickerbocker expanded up and down the East Coast, then the Midwest and West, opening more warehouses. As the category matured in the 1970s, smaller producers fell away and Knickerbocker became one of the top remaining producers. The company eventually narrowed its line to bed frames. In the 1990s, facing import competition, Knickerbocker built a strong internal R&amp;D program to make its frames a profit center for retailers rather than a commodity.

In the late 1990s, many retailers gave frames away or sold them cheap. The answer was **Eventide**—a support system that brought more style to the category. Designed to retail around $199, Eventide used a steel core from recycled railroad steel with composite resin, durable and good-looking, with a patented caster so beds could roll on many floor types, and tool-free assembly. Eventide had a long successful run.
