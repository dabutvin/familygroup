<h2 id="old-country">From the old country to Bayonne</h2>

Grandpa Isaac Aaron Sklar had been on his way to Argentina to join his wife's brothers, who had emigrated there. But his own brothers—Jacob (Yankiel) and Louis (Leybish)—had recently settled in Bayonne, New Jersey, and they wrote him letters urging him to come join them instead. He arrived in 1906 and moved in with Yankiel on Avenue C near 24th Street. Grandma Celia and all the children followed four years later, in 1910.

After the Zeyta left, Bubbe Celia moved in with her sister-in-law Liba and Liba's family across the river in Damur. She sold her fur-lined cape to get by. Isaac, meanwhile, had settled in with his brother Jacob at 540 Avenue C—he appears in the 1910 Federal census as "Iky Skla."

When it was time for the family to follow, they traveled by train to Libau in Latvia, a long journey. Each older child was responsible for a younger one; Uncle Max was Sophie's charge. Sophie faked her age and hid in the luggage racks and berths to avoid paying for a ticket. They sailed from Libau on the SS *Russia* on August 30, 1910, arrived in New York on September 11, and moved in with Louis and Dora Sklar. The ship manifest lists Zippa Schkliar, 40; Wulf, 16; Nechema, 14; Maria, 11; Schifra, 10; Liba, 7; Yael, 4; Mendel, 3.

<h2 id="russia">The Sklars in Russia</h2>

The Sklars came from Dnepropetrovsk in Ukraine, then part of the Russian Empire. The city lies on the Dnieper River, the third longest in Europe; at the time the family left, it was still called Ekaterinoslav. Liba Sklar—the Zeyta's sister—and his parents, Benzion and Mariasha Sklar, lived across the Dnieper in Danmur or Aframur. Benzion ran a lumber mill with many men in his employ. Liba and her husband ran a grocery store or flour mill nearby. It was a suburban area. Liba lived through World War I.

Benzion had a method for hiring young men at the mill: he invited them to lunch first. Whoever finished quickest got hired first—he figured those men would waste less of the workday eating.

Louis—Meyer Leibish Sklar, the youngest of Benzion's children—was the first to immigrate to the United States. Yankiel (Jacob) followed shortly after, and finally Isaac. Benzion's other children lived out their lives in Russia.

Lipa—Lyov, an older son—died in his late thirties of brain fever, probably before 1898. Uncle Leon Sklar, born that year, was likely named for him. Lyov had gone through the gymnasium and become a renowned scientist and inventor in Russia, carrying on an active correspondence with Thomas A. Edison at West Orange and Menlo Park, New Jersey, in the latter part of the 19th century. His work had something to do with musical sounds. He married a general's daughter; a representative of the Czar attended their wedding. They had four sons.

Moses and Noah were the two other children of Benzion and Mariasha. Noah passed away before Sophie left for America in 1910. Shifra Sklar—Benzion's mother—was the namesake for whom Sophie was named.

Before Celia, the Zeyta had fallen in love with another girl in his youth—beautiful, with long hair, a gorgeous voice, and a gift for the guitar. She had also been in love with someone else. They married, but after about a month her former lover returned and threatened Isaac's life. The marriage was ended.

Isaac and Celia lived in an apartment in Dnepropetrovsk. Grandpa Isaac was a skilled mechanic who repaired parts on the railroad. There were several pogroms, and he was forced to do guard duty. His brother Lipa's wife fled to the Zeyta's house with her four sons; they remained in Russia. One of those sons later became an officer in the Russian army and eventually made contact with the American relatives. The mother was a slim, attractive, well-dressed woman.

<h2 id="celia-family">Celia's Dunn family</h2>

Grandma Celia (Tsippa) Dunn Sklar had two older brothers—William and Zissel Dunn—and one younger brother, Samuel. They all lived in the same area.

One Pesach night, after the second Seder, William and his family were already in bed when they heard a noise. A man had broken in to steal a silver sugar bowl and creamer. William's wife grabbed the older daughter, Sonia, and escaped. William, trying to save the younger daughter, Rachel, was stabbed to death with a knife that had been left out on the table—killed on the spot, leaving his widow and two small girls. Later, his brother Zissel adopted Sonia and took his wife to Buenos Aires; they had no children of their own.

That night gave rise to the family's *bubbe-meise*: no matter how tired you are, every knife goes in a drawer after the meal. Never leave a sharp knife loose on the table.

Samuel, the younger brother, was drafted into military service and was miserable. He managed to position himself near a border crossing and slipped across a bridge into another country—probably Austria, Hungary, or Romania. From there he made his way to France, where he found work at Dreyfuss Co., the brokerage house. He married a French Jewish woman and they settled in Argentina. In the late 1940s, Samuel and his family visited the Sklar family group in Bayonne.

<h2 id="brothers-america">Louis, Jacob, and the brothers in America</h2>

Louis Sklar, also Gymnasium-educated, spoke Hebrew and Yiddish fluently and was a versatile musician—said to have perfect pitch. At 18 he was drafted into the Czar's Army, where he played first violin in the orchestra. On the side, he gave music lessons to children of local residents. One day a student's father warned him that trouble was coming for the Jews and offered to help. The man transported goods for the Russian Army—this was during the Russo-Japanese War—and at great personal risk, he smuggled Louis to the border in one of his trucks.

Louis had enough money to reach Frankfurt am Main, where he stayed four months, then crossed to England and settled in Petticoat Lane, the Jewish quarter of London. For six months he worked as a fish wrapper—wrapping fried fish in newspaper for customers to carry home—before leaving for the United States.

His first jobs in America were sewing men's trousers in a sweatshop and selling newspapers under the El. He was fired from the first one—it was a poor fit. Jacob, meanwhile, worked at a bedspring factory alongside a man named Mendel Miller. One day Mendel went to the docks to meet his arriving family—wife, five or six children, a sister, and a brother—and asked Jacob to bring Louis along. Louis was 20. Mendel's sister was a girl of 18 named Dora. Louis thought she was the most beautiful girl he had ever seen. They courted and married five years later. The brother, unfortunately, had a rash and was sent back to Europe.

Jacob Sklar was the second son to reach America. He had married Aunt Gitla in Russia; she died after giving birth to the twins Leon and Robert. He married again, to Aunt Bluma, and they had five children: Nathan, Lena (Leona), Eleanor, Oskar (Harry), and Benjamin. Jacob was a dapper man. After the bedspring factory he went into construction. By the 1920s he owned the Princess Apartments in Jersey City and the building at 84 West 25th Street in Bayonne, where many Sklars lived at one time or another.

<h3 id="metlife-pouch">Metropolitan Life pouch</h3>

A **Metropolitan Life** envelope pouch survives from the era—presented **To Isaac Sklar** with the compliments of **William Sklar**, a small artifact of father, son, and the insurance economy of the block.

![Met Life policy pouch — To Isaac Sklar, from William Sklar](/family-stories/metlife-pouch-isaac-william-sklar.jpg)

Louis Siegal and his wife Elizabeth, friends of the family, lived in Newark. They would come to Bayonne to visit, and one time they brought along their friend Dorothy Botwinick and introduced her to Uncle Nathan Sklar. Dorothy was 21 when they married, at the Clinton Manor in Newark on May 25, 1918. The following year they moved to 46th Street and Avenue C in Bayonne—the same house where Rita and Mickey Slootsky would live years later.

Aunt Mary met Uncle Sam Berlin through a *landsman* who had a tailor shop in Bayonne and had taken Sam on as a tailor. Mary went there with the Bubbe one day and met him. He asked her out; after two dates he proposed; she said yes, and they were married right away—April 1918, while she was living on West 24th Street.

Gussie Bernstein and Willie Sklar met through cousins.

Louis Polevoy was a friend of Willie Sklar's. Sophie first met him when she was 10; at 16 she had her first date with him, and they married about two years later. Louis had to ask the Bubbe's permission before he could even ask Sophie out, and they had to be home early. They would go to the Lyric Theater—later the Opera House on 26th Street and Avenue C—where ten cents bought a movie and five acts of vaudeville. Sophie was married in a rented gown: three dollars for the rental, and you kept the veil. The ceremony was at home, and the Bubbe made all the food.

There were four weddings in a single year: Willie and Gussie Bernstein in November 1917; Nathan and Dorothy on May 25, 1918; Mary and Sam Berlin on November 29, 1918; and Sophie and Louis Polevoy on December 25, 1918.

Becky Okun Rabinowitz was a cousin on the Zeyta's side. Her family had a jewelry store on Canal Street in New York. Itchik Chiam Okun was possibly Becky's father. She was a blood relative.

David Barkoff and his son Sidney lived in Borough Park, Brooklyn—also cousins.

Emmanuel Sklar, the second child of Willie and Gussie, died at age five. He had not been well from birth and had never spoken.

<h2 id="children-marry">Work, heartache, and the next generation</h2>

The Zeyta Isaac Sklar suffered a heart attack and moved in with Aunt Mary and Uncle Sam Berlin at 729 Avenue C in 1932, around the time Arnold Berlin was born. It was an ideal arrangement—Mary went to work, and there was a built-in babysitter.

Nathan, at 14, came down with a fever. The family lived close to Isaac and Celia at the time. A doctor was called, but it was too late—he died of appendicitis.

Harry Sklar became a dermatologist in Huntington, Long Island, and died in early middle age of heart disease. Ben Sklar, a severe diabetic, earned a doctorate in health care from the University of Michigan. He stayed in Michigan, married Hilda, raised two daughters, and served the state health department. Leona married Charles Rosenkrantz, who owned a candy store on Avenue C between 25th and 26th Streets on the east side of Bayonne. Charles collapsed and died of a heart attack on the night of Kay Sklar Furst's wedding. Their son Herbert ran an advertising agency in Jersey City for years before opening two mortuaries—Menorah Chapels—in Millburn and Teaneck. Herb was instrumental in bringing down the cost of the "Jewish funeral" in metropolitan New Jersey. His son Mark Ross went into the monument business; his daughter Karen became a mortician working alongside Herb.

Diabetes ran heavily in this branch of the family. Eleanor and her husband, Robert Freedman, were childless and lived for years next door to Leona Rosenkrantz at 84 West 25th Street. Gussie Bernstein Sklar spent her widow years in an apartment down the hall from Eleanor and Leona—the same building where the Sklar families had all lived in the 1920s.

Jacob Sklar died of a heart attack in 1935. Bluma died in 1940. The diabetic strain came from Bluma's side.

Sophie and Mary both had great respect for Miss Hayes, an early teacher who was kind to two pre-teen girls placed in a primary class because they knew no English. One of her first reading lessons: "Fanny has a fan, has Fanny a fan."

Grandpa Willie Sklar once went looking for a job and met a man who told him he needed a pair of overalls to get hired. The man offered to buy them for a price. Willie paid him and waited. No overalls, no money, no job. Later, Willie ran into the man again, demanded his money back—and got it.

Sophie and Mary went to work young—Sophie at 14, in 1914. Uncle Nathan found them jobs at a bookbinding factory in New York City. They earned five dollars a week: one for car fare, four for the Bubbe. Shirts cost two dollars apiece. Mary stayed at that job until she married. Sophie wanted more and kept looking—she got up to seven dollars a week before being laid off, then found work in Jersey City, six days a week.

<h2 id="marilyn-tree">Beatrice and Irving</h2>

**Beatrice Sklar**—one of Isaac and Celia's seven children—married **Irving Pinchuk** on June 7, 1925, in Bayonne. She was born in 1902 in Russia; he in 1897 in Russia or Poland. Their children were **Charlotte** (August 3, 1926), **Marilyn** (February 14, 1931), and **Marty** (January 1, 1940). Irving's parents were Mordechai Pinchuk and Chayasara, from Russia; Mordechai had a large family from two marriages, and Irving was one of many siblings. To the family, **Beatrice** and **Irving** were **MaBeatty** and **Pop**.

### "Are you a bird or a flower?"

It was something the family said to one another—a playful question with no wrong answer.

MaBeatty was a bird; she could sing beautifully. Pop was a flower. Charlotte, Marty, and Marilyn were flowers.

They vacationed in Miami Beach—walked the boardwalk, and she held his arm. He would never look at another woman. Once, a woman asked if they were together. MaBeatty pulled him close.

In Boca, MaBeatty loved to shop alone. They flew down and took the bus. She would hop on a bus to the shops; Pop, worried she'd get lost, caught the next one and followed her.

Artie and MaBeatty played gin rummy—loser took the trash out. Artie would lose on purpose. They had such a good time.

<h3 id="ace-thread">Ace Thread</h3>

**Irving Pinchuk** ran a thread-and-notions store in Manhattan called **Ace Thread**, at **72 Madison Avenue**. He carried a thread named for his wife and daughters—**BMC**: **B**eatrice, **M**arilyn, **C**harlotte. Irving had lived in **Brownsville**, Brooklyn, before meeting Beatrice; after they married, he moved to Bayonne.

<h3 id="bayonne-1940"><em>Bayonne Times</em> — "1940 Baby Derby"</h3>

On **Saturday, January 6, 1940**, the ***Bayonne Times*** ran a full-page **"1940 Baby Derby"**: local merchants offered gifts to the first baby of the year born to Bayonne parents. **Martin Pinchuk**—born **January 1, 1940**, at 10:45 p.m. at City Hospital to **Mr. and Mrs. Irving Pinchuk** of **106 West 48th Street**—was that baby. The page is a snapshot of Depression-era Bayonne retail: **Sklar's Kiddy Shop** at 519 Broadway and 24th, **Sam Henne** the wholesale grocer at 741 Avenue C, coal yards, furniture houses, and more.

<h2 id="snapshots">Marilyn and Fred's years</h2>

**Fred Butvinik** and **Marilyn Pinchuk** had three children in New Jersey:

- **Cheryl** — 1954 (Bayonne)  
- **Bonnie** — 1958 (Paramus)  
- **Warren** — 1960 (Paramus)  

**Warren Butvinik**'s Hebrew name honors **William Sklar**. He and **Kathy Dominick** had **Karen** (1986), **Jen** (1988), and **Dan** (1989).

They bought a house in Paramus, kept going back to the Shore, and stayed tied to Bayonne—where Marilyn had grown up and where the uptown shul and Avenue C still framed the family's streets.

**787 Pascack Road, Paramus, NJ 07652** — the house Marilyn and Fred bought in **1956 for $21,000**.

Belmar beach—people from Bayonne went to Bradley and Belmar every summer. *Evelyn Cohn (Butvinik), Joe Kohn, and Fred Butvinik.*

![Belmar beach — Evelyn Cohn (Butvinik), Joe Kohn, and Fred Butvinik](/family-stories/notes-belmar-beach.jpeg)

Ocean Grove once posted "No Jews" and chained the streets on Saturday nights so no one could drive through on Sundays—a remembered detail of the Shore in another era.

**124 West 49th Street** is where Marilyn grew up. She walked to Washington Elementary at 191 Avenue B in Bayonne. In grammar school she had to read from the New Testament and say the Lord's Prayer every day.

<h2 id="ohab-sholom">Temple Ohab Sholom</h2>

**Temple Ohab Sholom** was Bayonne's **"Uptown Synagogue."** Founded in **1915** as a religious society, it became a full congregation in **1919**, on **Avenue C near 48th Street**. Marilyn remembered it as **Ohab Sholem** at 1020 Avenue C between 48th and 49th, with a hall—the same uptown congregation, the shul her family knew.

![Typewritten minutes, Oct. 28, 1944 — window installed at Uptown Synagogue in memory of Isaac Sklar (stained glass, $100, children paid)](/family-stories/journal-family-group-1944-10-28-uptown-synagogue-window-isaac-sklar.png)

<p class="stories-image-caption">October 28, 1944 (23rd meeting — Dan and Hilda's).</p>

The synagogue closed and the building was sold in **2003**. It had been a center of Jewish life in Bayonne for generations.

<h2 id="knickerbocker">Knickerbocker &amp; the Polevoys</h2>

*Sophie Sklar married Louis Polevoy. Their sons and grandchildren built a bedding business that outlasted the horse-drawn era.*

Founded in 1919, Knickerbocker began with **Richard Polevoy's** grandparents, **Sophie and Louis Polevoy**, selling bed springs from a horse-drawn carriage in the New York metro area. In the 1950s their children expanded the line to folding beds, headboards, and metal bed frames—helping pioneer bed frames and support systems as a retail category.

Through the 1960s, Knickerbocker grew up and down the East Coast, then into the Midwest and West, opening warehouses along the way. As the market matured in the 1970s, smaller producers fell away and Knickerbocker emerged as one of the top remaining manufacturers. The company eventually narrowed its focus to bed frames. In the 1990s, facing import competition, Knickerbocker invested in internal R&amp;D to make its frames a profit center for retailers rather than a commodity.

By the late 1990s, many retailers were giving frames away or selling them cheap. Knickerbocker's answer was **Eventide**—a support system that brought style to the category. Designed to retail around $199, Eventide used a steel core made from recycled railroad steel with composite resin: durable, good-looking, with a patented caster for multiple floor types and tool-free assembly. It had a long, successful run.
